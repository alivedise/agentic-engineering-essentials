# [AEE-105] The Perception Gap

## Context

Engineers form opinions about AI capability from unrepresentative samples: polished demos, free-tier products constrained below commercial capability, media coverage optimized for impressiveness, and benchmarks designed for leaderboard rankings rather than production reliability. This produces systematic errors in both directions. The strongest available empirical evidence is the METR 2025 developer study: experienced open-source developers given access to early-2025 AI tools took 19% longer to complete tasks than the control group, yet believed afterward that AI had sped them up by 20–24%. Perception and reality diverged by approximately 40 percentage points in the same practitioners who were actively using the tools. Both underestimation and overestimation carry real costs in production systems.

## Design Think

The core claim: the perception gap has two failure modes — underestimation and overestimation — and each produces distinct, predictable engineering errors that can be avoided by grounding capability assessment in task-specific empirical testing.

**Underestimation** occurs when engineers dismiss AI for tasks where frontier agentic systems already perform reliably. The pattern: an engineer tried a consumer AI tool on a hard task, got a bad result, and generalized to "AI can't do this." The costs: missed productivity gains, delayed adoption, systems built without AI assistance that would benefit from it, and competitive disadvantage as peers adopt capabilities the team dismissed.

Underestimation is structurally reinforced by the sampling environment. Free-tier products are deliberately constrained below commercial-tier capability. Developer previews often ship at lower capability than production models. The media covers AI failures prominently. Benchmarks are frequently evaluated on versions of models that have since been superseded.

**Overestimation** occurs when engineers assume AI can handle tasks that require reliable judgment, safety-critical accuracy, or consistent performance on low-verifiability domains. The pattern: an engineer saw an impressive demo, assumed production reliability would match demo quality, and shipped without adequate evaluation. The costs: production failures, trust erosion among users and stakeholders, liability exposure in regulated domains, and expensive remediation.

Overestimation is structurally reinforced by the incentive environment. Demos are cherry-picked. Product announcements emphasize peak capability. Benchmark scores are reported on best-configuration setups. The gap between prototype performance and production performance is systematically understated. The Futurum analyst review of 2025 deployments documented "a big gap between agent prototypes and production-ready products" — a pattern consistent across the industry.

- Engineers MUST evaluate AI capability for their specific task, their specific model tier, and their specific deployment mode — not from general impressions, media coverage, demos, or benchmark leaderboards.
- Engineers SHOULD maintain a task-level evaluation log in their domain — model, configuration, task, success criterion, result, date — updated at least every 6 months, because capability curves shift.
- Engineers MUST separate capability assessment ("can this system do this task at the required reliability level?") from policy decisions ("should we automate this task?") — conflating the two produces both types of error: tasks are dismissed because they "shouldn't" be automated when they reliably can be, and tasks are automated because they impressively "can" be when policy should prevent it.

## Deep Dive

### Why the Gap Persists

The perception gap is not a failure of intelligence; it is a structural consequence of how AI capability is communicated and experienced.

**Demo optimization:** A demo is produced by selecting the best-performing inputs, the best-performing configuration, and the best-performing outputs from a distribution. The tail of the distribution — where production systems spend most of their time — is never shown. Engineers who form opinions from demos are forming opinions about the top percentile of a distribution.

**Free-tier constraints:** Consumer AI products are commercially incentivized to gate capabilities behind paid tiers. An engineer who concludes "AI can't do X" from a free-tier experience may be observing a deliberately constrained version of a system that handles X reliably at the commercial tier.

**Benchmark divergence:** Benchmark scores optimize for the metrics that define the benchmark. Production reliability on real tasks in real distributions is a different measure. A model that achieves 90% on a benchmark may achieve 60% on real production inputs for the same nominal task, because real inputs have distribution properties that benchmark designers did not anticipate.

**The METR finding in detail:** The METR empirical study is the sharpest available evidence for the perception gap. Experienced open-source developers — not novices — were given access to early-2025 AI tools and allowed to use them freely on real coding tasks. The treatment group took 19% longer than the control group. Yet when surveyed, treatment group members believed AI had sped them up by 20–24%. The self-report diverged from measured performance by ~40 percentage points. This is not about AI being bad at coding; it is about the difficulty of accurately perceiving tool impact when using a novel tool on familiar tasks. The takeaway for engineering teams: self-reported productivity improvements from AI tool use should be treated as hypothesis, not evidence, until confirmed by controlled measurement.

### The Cost Asymmetry

Overestimation typically produces more visible, faster-arriving costs than underestimation. A production failure from an overestimated AI system creates an immediate incident. Underestimation produces invisible opportunity costs: the system that was never built, the productivity that was never gained. This asymmetry means overestimation gets corrected faster but may cause more acute damage; underestimation persists longer but accumulates quietly.

## Best Practices

1. **Never form a capability opinion without testing the frontier model at the appropriate tier on your specific task with real production inputs.** Demos, benchmark scores, and general impressions are insufficient proxies. The only valid evidence is task-specific empirical testing against real data.
2. **Separate "AI cannot do this" (current capability fact, measurable) from "AI should not do this" (policy decision, a values and risk judgment).** These are different claims that require different evidence and different decision processes. Conflating them produces errors in both directions.
3. **Maintain a task evaluation log: model, configuration, task, success criterion, result, date. Revisit every 6 months.** Capability curves shift materially on that timescale. A task that a Tier 1 model failed in 2024 may be reliably handled by a Tier 1 model in 2025. Static capability opinions become stale and produce mis-calibrated systems.
