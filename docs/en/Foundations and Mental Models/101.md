# [AEE-101] The Agentic Capability Gap

## Context

There is a growing and widely misunderstood gap in AI capability perception. Engineers who form their views of AI from free-tier chat interfaces, older models, or casual use cases are working from an outdated and unrepresentative sample. This creates two groups speaking past each other: one dismissing AI as a novelty that fumbles simple questions, and one that has witnessed frontier agentic models autonomously restructure codebases over hours.

Understanding this gap is essential for making sound engineering decisions about when and how to invest in agentic systems.

## Design Think

AI capability is not uniform. It is stratified by:

1. **Model tier** -- free/deprecated models vs. frontier paid models differ dramatically in reasoning depth, instruction following, and agentic reliability
2. **Task domain** -- technical, verifiable domains (programming, math, formal reasoning) have improved far faster than soft domains (writing, advice, search) due to the availability of verifiable reward signals for reinforcement learning
3. **Mode of use** -- a model answering a one-shot question is a different system than a model given a terminal, long-horizon goals, and hours of runtime

Engineers MUST calibrate their expectations to the specific model tier, domain, and usage mode they are targeting. Generalizing from one configuration to another leads to systematic misjudgments.

The practical implication: if you have not yet used a frontier agentic model (e.g., Claude Code, OpenAI Codex) on a real engineering task with a real codebase, your mental model of AI capability is likely significantly out of date as of 2025-2026.

## Deep Dive

### Why Free-Tier Experience Is Unrepresentative

Most engineers encounter AI through free-tier products running deprecated or smaller models with aggressive safety filters, rate limits, and no tool access. These are optimized for broad, safe, cheap usage — not for agentic performance. The experience gap between a free-tier chatbot and a frontier model with tool access on a real engineering task is categorical, not incremental.

A concrete illustration from METR's 2025 empirical study: developers who had not used AI professionally expected a 24% productivity speedup from AI tools on a software task. Developers with professional AI experience expected roughly 20% and measured roughly 19% — but crucially, still believed AI sped them up even when their measured performance showed a slight slowdown. This illustrates two simultaneous failure modes: non-users underestimate AI, and experienced-but-not-expert users overestimate it while underperforming baseline.

### The Three Stratification Axes

The Design Think section identifies three axes (model tier, domain, mode of use). In practice these compound:
- A frontier model doing a one-shot question is weaker than the same model in a ReAct loop with tools
- A small model on a code task with verifiable feedback can outperform a large model on a writing task without feedback
- The same model with a well-designed harness outperforms the same model with a naive harness

### Calibration in Practice

The practical test for calibration: take a representative task from your domain, run it against a frontier agentic model with appropriate tool access, and measure. Form no capability opinion from demos, marketing copy, free-tier experiments, or benchmark scores for unrelated domains.

## Best Practices

1. **Calibrate against your exact deployment configuration.** Do not form capability judgments from model tiers, modes, or domains that differ from your target. A free-tier chatbot evaluation does not predict frontier agentic performance.
2. **Run frontier models on representative tasks before concluding AI cannot do something.** Many "AI can't do X" conclusions were reached on the wrong model, wrong mode, or wrong domain.
3. **Maintain a task-level capability log.** Track what you tested, which model and configuration, and what result you observed. Revisit every 6-12 months — the capability frontier moves faster than most engineering teams update their priors.
